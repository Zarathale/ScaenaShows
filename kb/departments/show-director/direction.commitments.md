# Direction — Commitments to Departments

> The Show Director's side of the intake conversation. This document formally acknowledges
> what each department has told Direction it needs, and articulates how Direction commits
> to working with each department across the Showcase Series and beyond.
>
> **Read this before writing any show brief.** It is the checklist of what Direction owes
> each department at intake — the information each department needs from Direction in order
> to do good work. Providing it upfront prevents post-authoring fixes and cross-department
> conflicts that could have been resolved at brief time.
>
> **Update this document** when: a department's brief-to-direction evolves, a recurring
> coordination problem reveals a gap in Direction's standing commitments, or a show's debrief
> surfaces something Direction should have provided at intake but didn't.

---

## Briefing Sequence — Sequential Dependencies

Some departments cannot begin without information that comes from another department. The
following sequence reflects those dependencies. Brief in this order or account for the
dependency explicitly.

1. **Set Director** — stage front orientation, mark definitions, environment. Almost everything else references marks.
2. **Casting Director** — entity types, AI intent, group size. Wardrobe and Choreography depend on this.
3. **Effects Director** — altitude arc. Fireworks and Camera need altitude and player position to design their work.
4. **Choreography, Wardrobe** — can proceed once Casting is complete and marks are defined.
5. **Fireworks, Camera** — can proceed once Effects has designed the altitude arc.
6. **Sound, Voice** — can begin with the brief's emotional arc; can refine once the full show timeline is roughed in.

When briefs are written in the wrong order, departments must guess at information they should have been given. That is a Direction failure, not a department failure.

## Sound — Direction's Commitments

**What Sound has told Direction it needs:**
The emotional arc of the show (two to three words). The world environment. Whether WEATHER or LIGHTNING effects are planned. The rough timing of major Voice moments. Any sections of deliberate silence.

**Direction commits to providing in every brief:**

*The emotional arc in words.* Before Sound can design an audio arc, Direction states the show's emotional shape: what the player moves through, and how. "Quiet unease to earned arrival" or "warm and wistful throughout" are both usable. Generic descriptions like "atmospheric" are not.

*World environment.* What biome, interior or exterior, and any significant ambient audio characteristics of the show's physical location that Sound would encounter before the show starts.

*WEATHER and LIGHTNING status.* A binary: yes or no for each. If WEATHER is planned, Direction notes it — Sound cannot stop the storm's audio bed and needs to design around it. If neither is planned, Direction says so.

*Major Voice moments — rough timing.* Direction tells Sound which sections or beats are Voice-primary — where text is the main instrument and audio should either support or clear. Direction does not need to provide exact ticks; sections or beats are enough.

*Deliberate silence sections.* If there is a section where Direction wants complete audio silence, Direction names it in the brief. An unmarked gap will look like a production error. Sound marks deliberate silence in the run sheet; Direction must first name it.

**What Direction acknowledges it may not provide upfront:**
Exact tick timing for all events. The full audio map emerges during authoring. Sound designs the arc from the brief's stated intent; tick-level coordination happens during the authoring session.

## Choreography — Direction's Commitments

**What Choreography has told Direction it needs:**
Full cast list with entity types. AI state intent per performer. All named marks defined. Player altitude arc (for aerial shows). Whether entrances, exits, and tableau moments are designed.

**Direction commits to providing in every brief:**

*The cast — from Casting.* Choreography cannot write a movement score without a cast list. The Casting brief is written before Choreography is briefed. The Casting brief confirms entity types and group size (solo, duo, chorus). Direction does not brief Choreography before Casting is resolved.

*AI state intent per performer.* For each figure in the show, Direction states whether the intended mode is puppet (controlled, precise) or performer (authentic AI, willingly unpredictable). Direction can state this as a preference; Choreography can push back if the creative intent and the entity type are in conflict. But Direction must state the intent rather than leaving it for Choreography to guess.

*Stage orientation and marks.* Direction does not brief Choreography before Set has defined the stage marks. Once Set's brief is complete, Direction includes the relevant marks by name in the Choreography brief — or points directly to Set's brief.

*Whether there is deliberate stage stillness.* If there is a section where nothing on stage should move — a tableau moment — Direction names it. Choreography writes stillness as deliberately as movement.

*Player altitude arc (if aerial elements are planned).* If Effects has designed a levitation sequence, Direction tells Choreography where the player will be at each major beat so performer positions can be authored relative to the player's actual location.

**What Direction acknowledges it may not provide upfront:**
Exact movement path descriptions (that is Choreography's creative contribution). Mark XZ coordinates (those come from Set, not Direction). Player altitude at exact ticks (that emerges from Effects authoring, not the brief).

## Wardrobe — Direction's Commitments

**What Wardrobe has told Direction it needs:**
Entity types confirmed by Casting. Emotional register for each figure. Any mid-show transformation moments.

**Direction commits to providing in every brief:**

*Entity types — from Casting.* Wardrobe is briefed after Casting. Direction does not ask Wardrobe to design a costume before confirming who is being dressed.

*Emotional register for each figure.* Not a costume description — a relationship description. "Tender companion," "ancient authority," "strange watcher," "absent presence." Direction provides this rather than letting Wardrobe guess what kind of show this is. A brief that only lists entity types without emotional register is an incomplete Wardrobe brief.

*Mid-show transformation moments.* If any figure undergoes a visible wardrobe change during the show — a change that is meant to communicate something about the character's arc — Direction names it at brief time. ENTITY_EQUIP is a transformation moment and needs to be designed, not added as an afterthought.

*Set palette, at minimum by register.* Direction notes whether the show environment is warm or cool-lit, light or dark, interior or outdoor. Wardrobe coordinates equipment palette against the visual environment; Direction gives enough context for that coordination to happen.

**What Direction acknowledges it may not provide upfront:**
Specific equipment choices (that is Wardrobe's craft). Whether the invisible-body technique should be used (Margaret proposes it; Direction approves it when it is a major show beat). Exact visual reads beyond the emotional register — "a knight" is acceptable brief language; "iron chestplate with a carved pumpkin helmet" is overclaiming Direction's authority into Wardrobe's territory.

## Effects — Direction's Commitments

**What Effects has told Direction it needs:**
The show's altitude arc. Whether aerial transcendence is designed and where in the arc. WEATHER and LIGHTNING status. Player starting location and environment. Any sections of deliberate physical stillness.

**Direction commits to providing in every brief:**

*The altitude arc.* The single most important input for Effects. Direction states, clearly, whether the player is grounded throughout; or whether there is a levitation or flight sequence; and if so, where in the arc the peak occurs. "Grounded throughout" is a complete answer. "Lift happens at the show's emotional peak, roughly two-thirds through" is a complete answer. "I'm not sure" is not — Felix asks before he starts, and Direction should know before briefing.

*WEATHER and LIGHTNING status.* Same commitment as to Sound. These affect the audio arc and the visual world simultaneously. Direction notes them explicitly rather than expecting Effects to find them in the timeline.

*Player starting position and environment.* Where the show begins spatially — outdoor terrain, indoor structure, a specific biome. This affects the starting point of any physical arc.

*Deliberate physical stillness.* If there are sections where the player's body should feel nothing — no levitation, no perceptual effects, no particles — Direction names them. Restraint from Effects is a contribution; Direction should author it intentionally, not by accident.

*Brief to Fireworks, before Effects is finalized.* Direction coordinates the timing of Fireworks and Effects briefs so both departments can agree on player altitude at pyrotechnic moments. This is not something Effects can resolve alone; Direction creates the conditions for the coordination.

**What Direction acknowledges it may not provide upfront:**
Exact ticks for altitude changes (those emerge from authoring). Fireworks burst altitudes (those depend on Fireworks design, which depends on Effects altitude — these must be worked out jointly). Camera orientation at liftoff (Camera is briefed separately; Direction ensures both departments know what the other is doing).

## Camera — Direction's Commitments

**What Camera has told Direction it needs:**
Stage front orientation. Named marks. Intended focus points at major beats. Whether cinematic perspective shifts are designed. Whether any free-look sections exist.

**Direction commits to providing in every brief:**

*Stage front orientation — compass direction.* Which direction is the show's "front"? This is the most basic spatial commitment Direction makes. Every Camera orientation call is relative to it. Direction names it explicitly: "The show faces south. The player is oriented toward the south when each major beat fires."

*Intended focus point at each major beat.* For each high-impact moment in the show — a firework burst, a performer entrance, a world transformation, the levitation peak — Direction names what the player should be looking at. "The player should see the firework burst overhead at the finale" is sufficient. "The player should notice the performer entering from stage left at beat 4" is sufficient. Camera decides how to make that happen; Direction says what the target is.

*Whether any sections are designed for free-look.* If there is a section where the player's freedom of attention is part of the experience — where looking wherever you want is the design — Direction flags it. Camera should not author orientation calls into a free-look section.

*Whether cinematic perspective shifts are intended.* If a moment calls for spectating a drone, riding a performer, or a screen blackout transition, Direction names it as a creative intent. Camera designs the mechanics; Direction confirms the intent is in the brief.

**What Direction acknowledges it may not provide upfront:**
Fireworks positions and altitudes before Fireworks has designed the show (Camera needs this to orient the player toward bursts). Exact tick timing for orientation calls (those are Camera's craft). The drone route for a smooth pan (that is Camera's design). Direction provides the focus point; Camera decides the method.

## Fireworks — Direction's Commitments

**What Fireworks has told Direction it needs:**
Number of pyrotechnic moments and their arc position. Color/palette direction. Emotional register per beat. Player altitude at each moment (from Effects).

**Direction commits to providing in every brief:**

*Number of pyrotechnic moments and where they fall.* How many firework beats does this show have, and are they early, at the peak, at the close, or distributed? This shapes Mira's entire design — she needs to know how many instruments she has and where to spend them.

*Color and palette direction.* Warm gold (ceremonial, earned), cool blue-green (wonder, strangeness), celebratory pastels or rainbow (abundance, community), or neutral? Direction makes this call as part of the show's visual language. Mira can work with any register; she cannot guess at it.

*Emotional register per beat.* For each pyrotechnic moment: arrival, finale, punctuation, celebration, threat? Direction names the emotional function of each beat. Mira selects the pattern type and density to serve that function.

*Player altitude — by coordination with Effects.* Direction does not require Effects to provide altitude to Fireworks independently. Direction creates the briefing conditions for the two departments to align: once Effects has designed the altitude arc, Direction passes that information to Fireworks, or schedules a joint briefing. Altitude mismatch is a Direction failure, not a departmental one.

**What Direction acknowledges it may not provide upfront:**
Pattern type (Mira chooses that from the emotional register Direction provides). Specific preset IDs (Mira selects from the library or proposes additions). Chase direction or power variation (those are Mira's compositional choices). Exact tick positions (those emerge from authoring).

## Voice — Direction's Commitments

**What Voice has told Direction it needs:**
Text density preference. Timing mode for each major physical event (before / with / after / silent). Deliberate silence sections. Opening register. Any specific lines or themes.

**Direction commits to providing in every brief:**

*Text density: talkative or listening.* One answer. This is the most important Voice input and the one most likely to be absent from briefs that aren't written with Voice specifically in mind. Direction states it as simply as possible: "This is a listening show — Sprite speaks three times, maximum" or "This is an active narration show — Sprite is present throughout."

*Timing mode for each major physical event.* For every significant physical beat in the show — a lift, an entrance, a firework, a transformation, a world change — Direction specifies: does Sprite speak before it, with it, after it, or not at all? This is a non-negotiable; no narration is authored without it. Direction writes the timing decision into the brief, not into the YAML.

*Deliberate silence sections.* If there are sections where Sprite should not speak regardless of what is happening, Direction names them. "Voice is silent from the levitation peak through the descent" is a brief statement. Sprite marks it as authored silence in the run sheet.

*Opening register.* How does Sprite address the player at the very start? Guide, witness, companion, oblique narrator? The first line sets the contract for everything that follows. Direction provides enough context to write an opening — or provides the first line itself if Direction has strong feelings about it.

*Any specific content commitments.* If there is a line, phrase, or theme that matters to Direction — something from the concept that should surface in Sprite's narration — say it in the brief. Sprite is given latitude, but not unlimited latitude. "The word 'door' should appear somewhere in the show's narration" or "Sprite should acknowledge that the player has been here before" — these are brief statements that Sprite designs around.

**What Direction acknowledges it may not provide upfront:**
Exact wording of any line (Sprite writes that). Text mode for specific moments (Sprite chooses MESSAGE vs. TITLE vs. ACTION_BAR; Direction says what emotional weight the moment carries). BOSSBAR color and section structure (Sprite selects based on the register Direction provides). The list of when Sprite's voice is in conflict with Sound — that is a run-sheet-level coordination resolved by Stage Management.

## Standing Cross-Department Commitments

*Things Direction commits to doing on every show, regardless of which departments are active.*

**Name deliberate silences.** In every department where silence or restraint is an instrument — Sound, Voice, Effects — Direction marks the intended silences in the brief rather than leaving gaps that look like oversights. "This section is quiet" is a brief statement. Unmarked silence is a production problem.

**Brief in sequence.** Set → Casting → Effects → (Choreography, Wardrobe, Fireworks, Camera) → (Sound, Voice). Direction does not brief Department B for work that depends on Department A's decisions before Department A has been briefed.

**Provide the arc.** Every brief begins with the show's emotional arc — what the player moves through, start to finish, in two to three phrases. This is the one piece of information that all departments use and that only Direction can provide.

**Confirm gaps upfront.** If a show depends on a capability that is currently gapped (variant/profession, STOP_SOUND by sound_id, etc.), Direction names it in the brief rather than letting departments discover it mid-authoring. The ops-inbox is Direction's resource for this; check it before finalizing any brief.

**Flag the altitude arc explicitly.** The most common cross-department miss is altitude mismatch — Effects, Fireworks, Camera, and Choreography all need to agree on where the player is at key moments. Direction creates the conditions for that agreement by flagging altitude-arc decisions in the brief before departments begin authoring independently.

## Calibration Roadmap

> Each department maintains a calibration backlog — specific capabilities they want to
> develop deeper mastery over. Direction's responsibility is to ensure those backlog items
> get real show exposure, not just demo exposure. Named patterns only become reliable
> instruments once they've been tested in a show with actual creative stakes.
>
> The roadmap below assigns calibration priorities to the upcoming Showcase Series and
> to demo rounds. It is a living plan — reassign items as show briefs develop and actual
> creative needs clarify which shows are the right homes for which calibration work.
>
> **Showcase status at time of writing:** All three shows are at Brief stage. Calibration
> assignments below are proposed, not locked. When a show's brief makes an assignment
> clearly right or clearly wrong, update this section.

Brian should treat C-level arrivals in showcase.03 as the calibration context for the arrival button, noting what registers as "musical confirmation of the moment" vs. "ambient noise."

### Voice Calibration Roadmap

**Backlog items (from `voice.kb.md §Calibration Backlog`):**

| Pattern | Status | Description |
|---------|--------|-------------|
| Timing modes calibration | 🔲 R7 finding | Before / with / after / silent — needs per-show directorial placement |
| `line.opening` | 📋 Proposed | The show's first address to the player |
| `line.transition` | 📋 Proposed | Bridge between major sections |
| `line.arrival` | 📋 Proposed | Accompanies or follows a dramatic entrance |
| `line.silence` | 📋 Proposed | The authored nothing — deliberate quiet |
| `line.close` | 📋 Proposed | The show's final address |

**Direction's roadmap:**

R7 identified that Voice timing — when Sprite speaks relative to physical events — was not specified in the brief and resulted in narration landing at wrong moments. This is a Direction failure as much as a Voice one: the timing modes (before / with / after / silent) are a Show Director call that must appear in every brief. The line types give Direction and Sprite a shared vocabulary for brief conversations.

- **All three Showcase shows** — timing modes are required for every show, not a calibration item that gets "assigned" to one show. Starting with showcase.01, every show brief must include timing mode specifications for each major physical event. Direction treats this as a standing practice, not an experiment.

- **showcase.03 "Welcome"** — the natural first test of `line.opening` and `line.close` as named roles. A welcome show has a clear opening address and a genuine send-off. Direction will use showcase.03 to confirm that briefing Sprite with "line.opening" and "line.close" as role labels (rather than writing the lines directly) produces narration that feels authored and intentional.

- **showcase.01 "The Cabinet"** — if The Cabinet has a performed entrance or arrival moment, it is the right context for `line.arrival` calibration: confirming that the timing direction (before/with/after) produces the intended effect on the arrival beat.

- **showcase.02 "The Long Night"** — likely to have the most transitions between states. `line.transition` is the right calibration target for a show organized around a long passage of time with shifting registers.

- **Priority order:** Timing mode discipline (all shows, immediately) → `line.opening` and `line.close` (showcase.03) → `line.arrival` (showcase.01) → `line.transition` (showcase.02) → `line.silence` (any show that has a deliberate Voice silence section).
