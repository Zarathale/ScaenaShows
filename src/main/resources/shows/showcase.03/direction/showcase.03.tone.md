# Tone — showcase.03 "Welcome"

**Core phrase:** *Warm, earned, celebratory — but not loud.*

---

## The Distinction This Show Hinges On

There is a version of "Welcome" that is a production. Confetti, fanfare, spectacle. The
player is an audience member at their own welcome party. That version is not this show.

There is a version of "Welcome" that is a feeling. The moment when a place becomes yours.
When the right person says the right thing and you know, for the first time, that you
belong here. That version is this show.

The test for every department decision: does this add to the feeling, or does it add to
the production? The production is always easier to build. The feeling requires restraint.

---

## What "earned" means

The Arrival must be earned by the Build. If the Build doesn't create genuine anticipation,
the Arrival is just fireworks and light. Departments should design their Build contributions
with the question: *what am I creating the conditions for?* The Arrival's impact is
proportional to what came before it.

---

## Per-Department Tone Translation

**Voice (Sprite)**
The welcome words are not an announcement. They are an acknowledgment. The difference:
an announcement says "something is happening." An acknowledgment says "you are here."
Sprite's Arrival line should feel like the thing a person says when someone they were
waiting for finally walks through the door. It is specific, quiet, and true.

The Coda line (if used) should feel like an exhale. Not a conclusion. Not a summary of
what just happened. The thing you say in the silence after.

**Sound (Gracie)**
The Build's audio should feel like a gathering — not a buildup of volume, but a buildup
of presence. More things arriving in the soundscape, not the same things getting louder.
The Arrival's sound is the one that says "yes" — whatever that means in Gracie's vocabulary.
The Celebration is full and real; the Coda should be what remains when you turn the music
off but can still almost hear it.

**Fireworks (Mira)**
Joy has a shape and Mira defines it here. The Celebration's fireworks should feel like an
expression of genuine feeling, not a display. The difference: a display fires evenly and
maintains energy. An expression has a peak and knows when to quiet. The arc matters more
than the individual bursts. What does it feel like when the last firework has fired and
you know it was the last one?

**Lighting**
The Build is warm. Expectant. The state of a room that knows someone is coming. The
Arrival shifts that state — not dramatically, but meaningfully. The Celebration is bright.
The Coda returns to something warmer and quieter than the Celebration but different from
the Build — the light of after, not the light of before.

**Camera (Mark)**
At the Arrival, the player needs to be looking at the right thing. Mark's decision is:
what IS the right thing? It might be the welcoming committee. It might be the sky above
the Welcome stage where Mira fires. It might be the threshold itself. Whatever it is,
the player should feel that they're seeing it at exactly the right moment — not oriented
by force, but by the feeling that this is where to look.

**Choreography**
The welcoming committee's movement during the Celebration should feel like people
expressing joy, not performers executing choreography. If it looks like a routine, it
has crossed into production rather than feeling. The question for Choreography: *what
do people actually do when they're glad someone is here?* Move toward them. Gather.
Maybe not in perfectly coordinated ways.

**Casting**
Who is on the welcoming committee? The answer shapes what welcome means on this server.
The entities Casting chooses should feel like residents — creatures or figures that
belong here, that are glad to have you. Not an impressive ensemble. A genuine one.

**Effects**
The physical sensation of Arrival should be unmistakable but not disorienting. The player
crosses the threshold and their body knows something has changed. Whatever Effects authors
here — a lift, a settling, a clearing — it should feel like the physical equivalent of
a door opening. Arrival is a change of state, not a dramatic event.

**Set**
The Welcome stage should feel like a place that was made to receive people. Not a stage
designed to look impressive — a space designed to make someone feel received. The approach
axis matters: the player should be approaching something. There should be a there there.
When Zarathale is scouting or building, the question is: *does walking into this space
feel like arriving somewhere?*
