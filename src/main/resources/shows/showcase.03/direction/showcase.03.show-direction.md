# Show Direction — showcase.03 "Welcome"

> This document governs all department decisions. Read it before making any creative call.

---

## Non-Negotiables

**1. The Arrival is one moment, not a sequence.**
Every department's contribution at the Arrival must land together. Not: fireworks, then
voice, then light shift. Together. If the Arrival sounds like departments taking turns,
it failed. This requires coordination at intake that is tighter than anything we have
done before. Every department must know the exact tick of Arrival and author to it.

**2. The player must feel specifically seen, not generically celebrated.**
"Welcome" is not the same as "Congratulations!" The distinction is Sprite's to carry —
the words at Arrival must address the player as a particular person arriving in a particular
place. If the line could appear at any show for any player, it is not the line. Write the
one that only belongs here.

**3. The Coda must be quiet.**
After the Arrival, something exhales. The show does not end at its peak. It ends in the
world that follows the peak. Whatever the Coda is — one Sprite line, an ambient bed, a
single Allay circling overhead — it must be small, unhurried, and specific. The temptation
to add more after the celebration will be strong. Resist.

**4. The welcoming committee arrives before the player.**
The committee does not respond to the player's arrival — they are waiting for it.
This distinction is everything: a welcome is something you are received into, not something
that assembles around you after you appear. Choreography and Casting must confirm the
committee is staged before the player's arrival tick.

**5. The Welcome stage is permanent.**
Set designs this location to outlast this show. Coordinate decisions (sight lines, ceiling
height, approach axis) with future-use in mind. This stage will appear in the actual
onboarding experience for new players. Every block and mark documented in the registry.

---

## Watch For

**The Arrival becoming overwhelming.**
Maximum simultaneous department output can produce noise rather than impact. The
Director will review the convergence moment before tech and compress anything that
competes rather than compounds. Departments should draft their Arrival contributions
knowing they will be evaluated for whether they add to the moment or muffle it.

**The Build dragging.**
The approach to the Arrival is the most exciting section to design, which means it is
the most likely to go long. If the Build takes longer than the Celebration, the balance
is wrong. Stage Management will flag this in the first run sheet. The Director will
prioritize this ratio at the Tech review.

**Fireworks and Effects misaligned on altitude.**
Mira's y_offsets are calibrated to the player's elevation. If Effects and Mira have not
agreed on the player's position before either authors, the Arrival will have fireworks
in the wrong place — either above the player (underwhelming) or at eye level (dangerous
feeling rather than celebratory). This agreement happens at intake. It does not happen
at revision.

**Sprite carrying too much.**
The Arrival is when the team finally has the player's full attention. The temptation is
to use all of it. Sprite's Arrival words must be one or two lines. Say the one true thing.
Trust that every other department is saying the rest.

**The Celebration outlasting its welcome.**
Joy has a duration. The Celebration section should be full and real but should know when
it's done. Mira's arc should have a shape — rise, peak, fall — not a sustained burst.
Sound should begin to open into the Coda before the last firework. The transition to quiet
is as important as the peak.

---

## Departments with Elevated Priority

**Effects, Fireworks (Mira), Camera (Mark) — Convergence leads**
These three must agree on the Arrival before any other department makes decisions about
it. The altitude, the facing, and the physical experience of crossing the threshold are
the armature everything else hangs on. Convergence leads brief to the Director before
other departments are briefed.

**Voice (Sprite) — Narrative lead**
Sprite carries the show's emotional argument. If the words don't land, the show doesn't
land regardless of what else works. The Arrival line and the Coda line are reviewed by
the Director before YAML is written. Voice has final say on the show's meaning.

**Choreography — The image of welcome**
The welcoming committee's arrival is the show's second-most important image, after the
convergence moment itself. Choreography needs the Welcome stage's spatial design before
they can block the committee's positions. Coordinate with Set at intake.

**Set — Permanent infrastructure**
The Welcome stage is the most important physical deliverable in the Showcase Series.
Zarathale designs and builds it with longevity in mind. The approach axis, the threshold
marker, the sight lines — all documented in the stage registry with future production
notes.

## Section-Level Direction

### Build — "The Overture"
*"Something is being prepared. The player can feel it."*

The player arrives before the celebration does. The Welcome stage exists but hasn't
committed yet. Departments layer in: Sound begins with something present but not full.
Lighting establishes an evening state — warm, expectant. Voice may speak one line that
names nothing but sets the register. Camera orients the player toward the welcome axis.

The welcoming committee is already there — Choreography has them staged before the
show begins. They are not performing. They are waiting. The player sees them; they see
the player. That mutual recognition is the beginning of the welcome.

The build has a tempo. It accelerates — not in speed, but in density. Each layer that
comes in makes the next arrival feel more inevitable. By the final thirty ticks before
Arrival, the player should feel something about to happen.

### Arrival — "The Threshold"
*"The world responds."*

The player crosses something — Set's threshold — and the show answers. This is the tick
where every department speaks at once:

- Effects: the body knows it has arrived (altitude shift, or one clear physical sensation)
- Fireworks / Mira: the sky responds (calibrated to player altitude — the contract from intake)
- Sound / Gracie: the sound of arrival (not a fanfare — the real sound of a door opening
  that's been waiting to open)
- Lighting: the world brightens or shifts — the environmental response to a presence arriving
- Camera / Mark: the frame that makes the Arrival visible (the one orientation the player
  needs to have)
- Voice / Sprite: the one or two words that make it human

All of this at once. Not in sequence.

### Celebration — "The Occasion"
*"Joy with a shape."*

The celebration is real and full. Mira's arc has its full expression here — rise, peak,
and fall. Casting's welcoming committee is present and alive. Choreography may move them
through the space but does not over-choreograph — joy does not move in neat patterns.
Sound is at its fullest. This is the only section of the show where fullness is the right
answer.

But the Celebration knows how to end. Sound begins opening into the Coda before the last
firework. Mira's final burst should feel like a finale, not a cutoff. The transition from
Celebration to Coda is the show's second-most delicate moment.

### Coda — "The After"
*"You're here. Good."*

One quiet thing. Sprite speaks the last line — the thing the welcome was always pointing
toward. Sound settles into the ambient bed of the Welcome stage as it will always be.
Lighting holds the state the world will carry when the show ends. The welcoming committee
may still be present or may have withdrawn — Casting and Choreography decide together, and
the decision says something about what welcome means on this server.

The player is left standing in the world. It is theirs now. The show ends before they do.
