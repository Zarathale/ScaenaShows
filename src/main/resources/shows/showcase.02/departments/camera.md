# Camera — showcase.02 "The Long Night"

## What This Department Serves

This show asks more sustained camera work from Mark than anything previously built.

There are no teleports — the player never leaves the location. Orienting the player across
10–12 minutes in a single space, without the natural reset that a teleport provides, requires
a different approach than an event-driven show. Mark must work with the space itself —
finding angles that reveal different qualities of the same location across the arc from
dusk to dawn.

The show direction identifies a philosophy question for this show that Mark must answer at
intake: **Does the player look INTO the storm, or does the storm arrive to find them?**
This is not a technical question — it's a framing question. The answer shapes the entire
camera approach to Section D (The Storm Breaks) and possibly to the sections before it.

---

## Per-Scene Direction

### A — Dusk Arrival
Camera establishes facing on arrival. The player should be oriented toward the most interesting
aspect of the space — whatever Set has identified as the arrival sight line from the stage
registry entry.

After the initial orientation: minimal intervention. The player is settling into a space
they'll be in for the full show. Let them look around. One initial `FACE` at arrival; then
release.

The facing at dusk should, ideally, be toward the horizon where light remains — the warm
western sky. If Set's coordinates allow this, the opening image is the last of the day's color.

### B — The Night Deepens
No camera intervention during the deepening night. The world handles itself — the TIME_OF_DAY
shift is atmospheric, not event-driven. Mark watches and reserves camera energy for later
sections.

One possible exception: if Choreography has placed a nocturnal creature (Bat, Wolf at distance)
that is briefly visible and worth orienting the player toward, a very gentle `FACE` could
direct attention. This is optional and should only be used if the specific creature and its
position make it genuinely worth seeing. Do not over-use the instrument in the patience sections.

### C — Something Builds
The weather changes (Lighting's decision). The show direction says: "Camera does not call
attention to the change — the player should notice before they're told to notice."

This means: no FACE events toward the sky, no reorientation toward the storm's approach.
If the player is already oriented toward interesting sky, leave them there. If not, Mark's
restraint in this section is itself a camera choice.

The pressure builds without being pointed at.

### D — The Storm Breaks
Mark's primary answer: **looking into the storm, or found by it?**

**Option A — Looking into it:** Before the LIGHTNING fires, Mark issues a `FACE` that
orients the player toward the sky above the strike location. The player is watching when
it happens. The LIGHTNING arrives at something they're already facing. Register: witness,
presence, met by the storm.

**Option B — Found by it:** No camera adjustment before the strike. The LIGHTNING fires
into whatever the player is looking at — or in their peripheral vision, or behind them if
they've turned around. They are not watching the sky when it happens; the storm arrives to
find them. Register: shock, interruption, the world acting without notice.

Both are valid aesthetics. Mark brings a recommendation to intake — not a preference, a
recommendation with reasoning.

If Option A: the `FACE` fires 60–120 ticks before the LIGHTNING tick. Enough warning that
the player registers the orientation; not so much that it telegraphs the event.

If Option B: no FACE before the strike. Possibly a FACE immediately *after* — if the player
needs to find the strike's aftermath, Mark redirects them within 10–20 ticks of the flash.

### E — The Aftermath
The storm has passed. If camera did not redirect after the strike, now is the time — toward
the sky, toward the world in its storm-altered state. Or: not. The player has already turned
to look; do not assume they need direction.

One possible camera gesture: in the aftermath's quiet, a slow `FACE` that brings the player
from wherever they are to a view of the clearing sky, or the horizon, or the location's
environmental anchor. This is the resting place before dawn. The player should have a view
they're comfortable holding for a long quiet section.

### A' — Dawn
Camera's final decision: does the player face east (toward the horizon where dawn comes) or
hold the view they've been in through the aftermath?

Option 1 — Face the dawn: a `FACE` toward the eastern horizon just as the TIME_OF_DAY shift
toward dawn begins. The player watches the world get lighter. The Sprite dawn line arrives
while they're watching the light.

Option 2 — Hold the current view: no camera adjustment. The player is wherever they've settled.
The dawn comes to them. Consistent with Option B philosophy from the storm section.

Mark's philosophy choice at Section D should inform this decision. A consistent camera
philosophy across the show is more powerful than a series of optimal individual framings.

## Show-Level Constraints

- This show is one location, 10–12 minutes. Camera restraint is the instrument — the number
  of `FACE` events should be low and intentional.
- FACE events during the storm section (C and D) are the most consequential. Each one commits
  to the philosophy or abandons it.
- Mark reads the full show arc before intake — not just the camera-tagged sections. A camera
  philosophy for this show requires knowing what the show is about.
- Coordinate with Sound on the LIGHTNING tick so Mark knows exactly when to time any pre-strike
  `FACE` (if Option A is chosen).

---

## Intake Questions for Camera

1. **Storm philosophy:** Option A (looking into it) or Option B (found by it)? Bring reasoning.
2. **Arrival facing:** Does Set's registry entry produce the right initial orientation
   (ideally toward the western sky at dusk)? Or does Mark need to adjust with a `FACE`?
3. **A' dawn:** Face the horizon (Option 1) or hold current view (Option 2)? Consistent
   with the storm philosophy?
4. **Restraint inventory:** Across the full arc, how many `FACE` events does Mark actually
   need? List them by section at intake. Low is better.
