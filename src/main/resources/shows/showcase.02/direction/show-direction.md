# Show Direction — showcase.02 "The Long Night"

> This document governs all department decisions. Read it before making any creative call.

---

## Non-Negotiables

**1. The A' section (dawn) must feel earned, not automatic.**
It is not enough to shift TIME_OF_DAY and call it a reprise. Something must register as
different about the world — in the sound, in what is or isn't present, in how the space
carries itself after the night. Departments must make a specific decision about A' that is
not just the A section played back in reverse. If A' feels like a reset, the show has
not landed.

**2. LIGHTNING fires once.**
Once. It is not repeated for effect, not used decoratively, not multiplied because one
didn't feel like enough. Everything before it is building toward it. Everything after it
is aftermath. The entire arc is organized around that single event. If it fires twice, the
show's structure is broken.

**3. Sprite speaks twice. No more.**
Once at the storm's peak — the thing the night says. Once at dawn — the thing the morning
reveals. All other words are silence. If those two lines are not strong enough to carry the
show's meaning, the lines need revision. The solution is not more lines.

**4. Lighting owns the arc. Conflicts resolve in Lighting's favor.**
This show exists to let Lighting exercise all three instruments across a full arc. When
any department's choice conflicts with Lighting's stated direction, Lighting wins — unless
the conflict is surfaced to the Director before authoring. No department should author
anything that fights the light without clearing it.

**5. The show takes place in one location. The player does not leave.**
No teleportation. No set changes. The world comes to the player. If a department's idea
requires moving the player to a different space, the idea is not for this show.

---

## Watch For

**Sound and Lighting competing during the storm.**
Sound amplifies the LIGHTNING event — thunder follows the flash. Coordinate the exact tick
of LIGHTNING with Sound before either department writes YAML. The flash happens first;
Sound confirms the hit. They are not simultaneous — they are causal. Get the offset right
at intake.

**Camera during the storm.**
FACE snaps in a storm can feel jarring rather than dramatic. Mark's question: does the
player look INTO the storm, or does the storm arrive to find them? These are different
camera philosophies. The Director will need an answer from Mark at intake — don't defer it.

**Choreography's nocturnal creatures.**
They are atmosphere, not story. A Bat flock overhead, a Wolf on a distant patrol — these
are part of the night, not characters in it. If a creature starts to feel like it's trying
to be interesting in its own right, pull it back. The night's creatures exist to deepen
the environment, not to provide narrative.

**The A' dawn arriving too deliberately.**
Dawn in the real world arrives before you notice it. The show's A' should have that quality.
Do not announce the dawn with a dramatic event or a Sprite line that says "dawn is here."
Let the light change and the sound change and let the player realize — oh, it's lighter now.
The Sprite line at dawn is a reflection, not an announcement.

**The storm section dragging.**
The build to the LIGHTNING peak needs to feel earned, not prolonged. The risk in a patient
show is lingering past the right moment. Lighting and Sound should agree on the storm's
duration at intake and hold each other to it.

## Departments with Elevated Priority

**Lighting (Show lead)**
This is Lighting's show. Full arc across all three instruments. Every other department
reads Lighting's plan before making decisions. Lighting briefs Lighting's arc to the
Director before any department briefing is issued.

**Sound / Gracie (Co-lead)**
Gracie and Lighting co-design the storm peak before either briefs separately. The audio
arc from dusk ambient to night to storm to aftermath to dawn is Gracie's primary exercise.
Sound and Lighting must be in agreement before Stage Management writes a tick.

**Camera / Mark (Secondary lead)**
This show asks more sustained camera work from Mark than anything previously built.
The challenge: orienting the player across long environmental stretches, not just at
event moments. Mark should receive the full show arc before intake, not just his sections.

## Scene-Level Direction

### A — Dusk Arrival
*"The world is settling."*

The player arrives. TIME_OF_DAY is early dusk — the world still has color. The ambient
sound bed is evening: what this biome sounds like at the end of the day. The player
needs a moment to orient before anything demands their attention. Camera establishes
their facing on arrival. Then the show simply begins living around them.

No Sprite line here. The arrival is the line.

### B — The Night Deepens
*"Something that was there all along begins to be more itself."*

TIME_OF_DAY progresses toward full night. The ambient bed shifts to night sounds.
Casting may introduce nocturnal presence — above (Phantoms, Bats), at distance (Wolf
patrol), in the underbrush. These are not events the player interacts with. They are
the environment deepening. Choreography keeps creature movement minimal and atmospheric.
Nothing approaches the player.

Sound layers beneath what is already present. Gracie does not introduce new elements
abruptly — she adds texture to what was always there.

### C — Something Builds
*"The air changes before the weather does."*

WEATHER event activates — rain or heavy cloud, Lighting's call. The sound shifts to
include rain if applicable. The atmosphere begins to feel pressured. No dramatic events
yet. The build is in the environment, not in the show. Camera does not call attention
to the change — the player should notice before they're told to notice.

### D — The Storm Breaks
*"It comes when it was always going to come."*

LIGHTNING fires. This is the show's peak. Sound confirms the hit — thunder, immediate
aftermath. Effects may introduce one brief disorientation if it serves the lightning's
physical weight (slow_falling or darkness for 2–3 seconds maximum). Camera's question
was answered at intake: does the player face into it, or does it find them?

Sprite speaks here. The line must be the one true thing the storm says. Not a description
of the lightning. Not a warning. The thing the night has been trying to say.

Fireworks are not used during the storm. The lightning is the event. Mira sits this
section out.

### E — The Aftermath
*"Quieter than before. That's the weather's doing."*

The storm passes. WEATHER may clear or may leave residual cloud — Lighting's call.
The sound settles. What was present before the storm may or may not still be present.
Departments may make a choice about the aftermath's atmosphere that differs from the
pre-storm night. The show is not over; it's in recovery. The player waits.

### A' — Dawn
*"It was lighter before you noticed it was lighter."*

TIME_OF_DAY shifts toward early dawn. Sound changes to morning ambient — birds, if
appropriate to the biome, or simply a shift in the night bed's quality. The A' does
not mirror A — it echoes it. The same place, but carrying what happened.

Sprite speaks here. One line. The line must be the thing the morning reveals — not the
thing the player already knows. Something that lands because of everything that came before.

Camera makes no adjustment unless the dawn requires it. The world is enough.
