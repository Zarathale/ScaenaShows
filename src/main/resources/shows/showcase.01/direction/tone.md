# Tone — showcase.01 "Preparing for Battle"

**Core phrase:** *Devoted, spare, slightly over their head but fully committed.*

This document elaborates that phrase per department. The Director does not prescribe
mechanics — departments own the translation. This document tells you what the tone
phrase means in your domain so you can find it yourself.

---

## What "devoted" means

The companion has decided this Hero is worth six expeditions and doesn't revisit the
decision. There is no scene where they wonder if it's worth it. There is no moment of
doubt. The commitment is total and quiet. The show does not celebrate the devotion — it
simply lets you see it. A department that tries to make the devotion touching will overshoot.
A department that trusts the player to feel it will land exactly right.

## What "spare" means

This show earns its beats by not spending them everywhere. One Sprite line per scene,
sometimes none. Lighting that sets and holds. Sound that scores the place, not the emotion.
Every department should ask: *am I adding what this moment needs, or what I can do?* The
answer "I can do this here" is not a reason to do it. The answer "this moment needs this"
might be.

## What "slightly over their head but fully committed" means

The companion is not a warrior. They are doing a warrior's provisioning. There is a soft
absurdity to this that the show does not explain and should not amplify. A small armorer
carrying a chestplate that is visibly too heavy for them is funny. A small armorer who
monologues about carrying a heavy chestplate is not. The register comes from understatement.
Do the thing. Don't comment on doing the thing.

## Per-Department Tone Translation

**Sound (Gracie)**
Each location has a sonic identity that is true to where it actually is. A forge sounds
like a forge — the ambient character is functional, not atmospheric wallpaper. The weapon
scene (Scene F) is the show's most emotionally weighted stop; Sound holds that space with
restraint, not score. The boots scene (E) is tactile — let the terrain speak. The home
base is a workshop: specific, warm, slightly cluttered in sound. Each A-section return
should feel like coming home to the same room.

The finale has no scored music unless you can make it feel like the room changed on its
own. If Sound is present in the reveal, it should feel like the world recognized what just
happened — not like a fanfare was cued.

**Lighting**
Home base has a consistent, warm quality — a craftsperson's space at work. It does not
change across returns. The player re-enters the same light each time and recognizes it.

Each expedition site has its own light logic native to that terrain. The forge is orange
and deep. The helmet's height location has the quality of open sky. Lighting does not
impose a unified palette across scenes — it respects where each location actually is.

The finale may warrant a single lighting shift: not dramatic, not theatrical. The quality
of something arriving into the light that was always meant for it. If Lighting can find
that, deploy it. If it doesn't feel exactly right, hold and let the mob visual do the work.

**Voice (Companion)**
The companion is writing a mission log, not performing for an audience. Their lines are
field notes: specific observations about the task at hand. What they found. How it was
heavier than expected. That this is the third time they've been to this forge. That the
shield was exactly where someone said it would be.

They do not editorialize the Hero. They do not say "I hope this is enough." They say
specific things about specific pieces. The emotional weight comes from accumulation, not
from any single line being profound. The best lines in this show are the ones that could
be in a practical procurement log and still make you feel something.

Silence is a voice choice. On some returns, the companion says nothing. The armor stand
records the expedition. That is sufficient.

**Casting**
The companion's species and design affect how the devotion reads. A small mob doing large
work has a particular register. An armorer villager is the most literal choice and
potentially the most resonant — their professional competence makes the devotion feel
purposeful rather than naive. Casting should propose alternatives with this logic in mind:
what species, in this role, makes the scale of the task feel both real and slightly absurd
in exactly the right proportion?

The Hero is cast for the reveal. They should look, when fully equipped, like someone the
six expeditions were worth. Not the most powerful mob in the game — but a mob that, in
full kit, reads as *ready*. There is a difference between impressive and worthy. Casting
should find worthy.

**Wardrobe**
The armor set is designed before anything else. It is a unified kit — not six random
pieces, but a deliberate aesthetic statement about who the Hero is going into. Design
the set first. Then each expedition site can be informed by what piece it holds.

The companion may have a functional appearance that reads as "armorer" — apron, tools,
something that says this is their profession. Or they may be visually unremarkable, which
lets their actions carry everything. Wardrobe proposes; Casting confirms. The companion
should not look more interesting than the Hero.

**Camera (Mark)**
Arrival facing at each expedition site should put the terrain's most relevant feature in
frame — height for the helmet site, depth for the forge, the path ahead for the leggings
site. The orientation choice is a curatorial one: how you look at a place says something
about what you're supposed to find there.

At the finale, Camera's job is to make sure the player is looking at the right thing when
the Hero arrives. The companion and armor stand should be in a clear sightline to the
reveal. If the Hero enters from a direction the player isn't facing, a single FACE redirect
is warranted — timed to the instant of arrival, not after.

**Effects**
Effects are present in this show only where the location's physics suggest it. Near-ground
levitation at the magma or snowfield boots site, if Set confirms terrain. A brief altitude
shift at the helmet's height location, if the verticality of the site warrants it.

Effects should never read as "we levitated you because we can." They should read as "the
ground here is different." If Effects cannot make that case for a given scene, they are
not in that scene.

**Fireworks (Mira)**
Mira is not guaranteed every expedition. The most likely home for fireworks is either the
shield scene (Scene G) if the location has open sky, or the finale reveal. Not both —
picking one is the right call.

If the finale: the Hero arriving into a small upward burst — warm, contained, not a
celebration but an acknowledgment. Or nothing. The visual of a fully equipped mob may not
need any fireworks to land. Mira proposes; Direction decides after Casting confirms the Hero.

If Scene G (the shield site): a small departure burst as the companion turns for home,
knowing they have everything. This would be the show's most emotionally earned fireworks
moment — the last expedition, the threshold, the turn toward home.

**Choreography**
The companion's movement at each site is purposeful. They arrive, assess, take the piece,
leave. Choreography stages this without commentary. No lingering, no dramatic pausing —
the work is the show.

At home base, the companion's presence near the armor stand is the show's visual anchor.
Choreography keeps them in relationship to it — not performing, just present. The armor
stand should always be in frame when the companion is at home.

**Set**
Six new locations. Each one scouted with its armor slot as the creative lens. The question
per location is not "is this impressive" — it is "does this feel like where this piece
lives?" A place that has height belongs to the helmet. A place that has heat belongs to
the chestplate. A place that has distance belongs to the leggings. The logic is poetic,
not literal — the forge doesn't have to have a crafting table in it. It has to have the
feeling of one.

Home base is a craftsperson's space. Functional. Specific. The armor stand is the
centerpiece. The player should understand at a glance that this is a workshop, not a lobby.
