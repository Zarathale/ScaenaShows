# Tone — showcase.01 "The Cabinet"

**Core phrase:** *Quietly eccentric, wonder-forward.*

This document elaborates that phrase per department. The Director does not prescribe
mechanics — departments own the translation. This document tells you what the tone
phrase means in your domain so you can find it yourself.

---

## What "quietly eccentric" means

The collection is not trying to impress you. It's not a theme park. It's not a gallery
opening. Someone has gathered these things because they found them genuinely remarkable —
and they're showing you because they think you might find them remarkable too. If it is
not shown with the same care it would be shown to the collector's most beloved guest,
it is not right yet.

"Eccentric" does not mean random. The collection has a sensibility — the Allay chose
these things for reasons. The logic of the choices might not be immediately apparent,
but it is not arbitrary. Every exhibit belongs.

## What "wonder-forward" means

Wonder does not require explanation. The show should make the player want to lean in —
not because they were told something is interesting, but because something actually is.
If a department is working to convince the player that an exhibit is remarkable, the
exhibit may not be remarkable enough. Find a more remarkable moment or a more honest
presentation of it.

## Per-Department Tone Translation

**Sound (Gracie)**
Think of the audio style of a natural history documentary where the music notices things
the narration never says out loud. The ambient bed for each location should sound like
where it actually is — not like "ambient background music for a fantasy space." The nether
sounds like the nether. The End sounds like the End. The comedy beat's timing is all
pacing and restraint — the funniest sounds are the ones that arrive one beat later than
expected.

**Lighting**
Each exhibit space has its own light logic. The collection's beauty is that each place has
been preserved in its own atmosphere. Lighting does not impose a unified palette across
vignettes — dusk in the Still Water vignette, the nether's native glow in Vignette D. What
Lighting ensures is that the home base has a consistent, slightly warm, slightly strange
ambient state that the player re-enters each time and recognizes. The A section is the one
place where Lighting makes a consistent choice.

**Voice (Sprite)**
The Allay doesn't have words. Sprite has field notes. They are sparse. Observational.
Slightly amazed — but not gushing. A field note that works: *"Found in a basalt delta.
The sound it makes has no known cause."* A field note that doesn't work: *"This incredible
creature will amaze you with its behavior!"* Sprite is a naturalist, not a tour guide.
The difference: a naturalist describes what they observed. A tour guide tells you what
to feel. Sprite describes. The feeling is the player's to have.

When Sprite is silent, it is because the exhibit is sufficient. Silence on a return to
home base means: *that needed no annotation.*

**Casting**
The creatures are not performing. They are being what they are. That is what makes them
remarkable. An Axolotl being an Axolotl — floating, passive, occasionally blinking — is
the exhibit. Do not stage it to be more interesting than it is. The collection's premise
is that these things ARE interesting. Trust that. If a creature needs help being interesting,
it may be the wrong creature for this collection.

**Wardrobe**
The collection may have a visual language — the Allay may have marked its collected
creatures in some way, or their appearance may simply reflect what they are, pristinely.
The question Wardrobe asks: *does this creature look like it belongs in a collection, or
does it look like something that wandered in?* Both are valid choices. Make one of them
deliberately.

The player's appearance does not need to change for this show. If it does change in a
specific vignette, the reason must serve the exhibit, not announce a capability.

**Camera (Mark)**
Mark orients the player toward each exhibit on TP arrival — that is the minimum. The
richer interpretation: each orientation choice says something about how you're supposed
to look at this thing. Looking UP at the Suspension exhibit is different from looking
ACROSS at the Still Water. The angle is a curatorial decision. What does the exhibit
look like from here, and does that angle make it more remarkable?

**Effects**
The player's physical sensations in this show should feel like the environment they're
in — not like plugin capabilities being demonstrated. Near-ground levitation in the Nether
should feel like the Strider's environment affecting the player, not like a levitation
demo. The test: if the player were to notice the effect as a game mechanic rather than
as an experience, it has stepped outside the collection's logic.

**Fireworks (Mira)**
Vignette E is Mira's exhibit. The collection contains this — a suspended place where
fire happens below you. The fireworks are the exhibit. They are not punctuation for
something else; they ARE the thing worth collecting. Mira should design this vignette
from the premise: *what does it mean to be above the fireworks rather than below them?*

**Choreography**
The creature theater (Vignette C) is the only vignette where Choreography has substantial
work. The blocking objective: get the entities onto the stage and hold them there. The AI
takes over. If choreography is still happening AFTER the entities are staged, it may be
pulling focus from the AI behavior — which is the actual exhibit.

**Set**
The collection is real places. Set's job is to find places that are worth collecting —
locations that have something about them that isn't immediately explainable but that you
notice. The scouting criteria: *would the Allay have found this remarkable?* Not: *is
this an impressive build?* Natural terrain with an unusual angle, a space where the light
behaves oddly, a structure whose purpose is unclear — these are collection candidates.
The contraption in Vignette F must already be a working redstone build before the
vignette is authored. Zarathale builds or finds it.
