# Show Direction — showcase.01 "The Cabinet"

> This document governs all department decisions. Read it before making any creative call.
> It is updated when the arc changes significantly. Check the stage field above.

---

## Non-Negotiables

**1. Home base must have a personality.**
The A-section is not a lobby. The Allay and at least two of its collected creatures must
be present and distinct at the opening. The player should feel welcomed into something
specific — a real place with a sensibility — not staged in front of a tutorial. If the
home base feels neutral, it has failed.

**2. Every vignette is complete in itself.**
No vignette sets up another. If any single vignette were removed, the others must still
work. The collection's logic: remarkable things do not need to explain each other. Departments
must not borrow from adjacent vignettes to complete their work.

**3. The comedy beat (Vignette F) lands through coordination, not explanation.**
Sprite does NOT name the joke. Sound calls the timing. Lighting and Casting do the rest.
Restraint is the joke — the contraption is small, the punchline is specific, the beat is
one hit. If the punchline needs narration to read as funny, it is not ready. The Watch
question: "did it land without being told to?" If no — revise the coordination, not
the narration.

**4. Every A-section return must be differentiated.**
The home base is the same place each time, but the return cannot feel identical. Sprite's
field note on return — or deliberate silence — must give each return its own character.
Four identical returns is a structure problem, not a style choice.

**5. Every stage used in this show enters the permanent stage registry.**
No exceptions. Set documents each location in `kb/departments/set/stage-registry.md`
before the show enters tech. These stages outlast this showcase.

**6. Set is the first mover. Nothing else gets authored until Set delivers.**
Department briefings will be issued before stages are confirmed — departments can plan.
But no YAML is written until Zarathale has scouted and documented real-world coordinates
for all six locations (home base + B through F). The YAML depends on actual sets.

**7. The show annotates; it does not narrate.**
Sprite speaks field notes, not tour narration. Departments establish sensation and then
step back. The player is trusted to feel what they feel. Every department should ask:
am I adding something the show needs, or something I can do? This show is built from
restraint.

---

## Tonal Register

The show's voice is a natural history documentary played completely straight. A small,
eccentric creature has assembled a collection of remarkable things. The show presents them.
It does not explain them.

The Allay collects. Sprite interprets. Neither explains.

The humor — and there is some — comes from the gap between the show's gravity and the
scale of what it finds remarkable. A contraption that does one small thing. A creature
that was waiting in exactly the right place. This register only works if every department
plays it straight. The moment a department winks at the player, the show loses its hold.

Every space the player visits is something the Allay found and thought worth keeping. Set's
job — and by extension, the show's — is to make the player feel that. A place doesn't
need to be grand. It needs to feel chosen.

## Watch For

**The Allay becoming precious.** Sprite does not narrate the Allay's feelings or intentions.
The Allay shows; Sprite observes. Lines like "the Allay is happy to see you" or "it has
been waiting" cross the line. The Allay is not a pet. It's a curator.

**Vignette length creep.** Each exhibit does ONE primary thing. If a department is adding
a second or third beat, the question is: does this serve the exhibit's primary thing, or
does it complicate it? The answer must be asked out loud and answered before authoring.

**Vignette B over-intervention.** Multiple departments have nothing to do in Vignette B —
and that's correct. B is the show's breath. Any event added to B because it "feels empty"
is misreading the vignette. B works precisely because the show steps back. Resist the
impulse to fill it.

**The creature theater vignette (C).** The zombie/villager interaction may or may not
produce a fight — Minecraft AI is not guaranteed. Design the vignette so that either outcome
is interesting. The Watch question: *what did the creatures do?* Not: *did the fight happen?*
If the vignette only works when the fight happens, it is underdesigned.

**The altitude/facing coupling in Vignette E.** Effects, Fireworks, and Camera are
co-designing one moment. The altitude number, the camera orientation philosophy, and
Mira's fireworks palette are coupled decisions — none of the three can be finalized without
the others. Gate 2 in Stage Management's intake order exists for this reason. Revision
pressure will try to pull these apart. Hold the gate.

**BOSSBAR consistency.** If used as a collection-progress indicator, it must appear for
all five vignettes with identical behavior. A BOSSBAR that appears in three vignettes and
not the others reads as broken. Either commit to it for the full show or don't use it.

**Vignette D (Nether) and Effects' levitation range.** The nether floor is lava-adjacent
in many biomes. If near-ground levitation is used, Set must document the safe altitude
ceiling for the chosen location. Effects must know this before authoring. Coordinate at
intake, not at revision.

## Departments with Elevated Priority

**Set (First mover / Primary)**
All YAML depends on Set's scouted coordinates. Zarathale executes the stage survey.
Set also owns REDSTONE in Vignette F — the contraption must be built in-world before
that vignette can be authored. Set briefs before other departments begin authoring.
Note: mob containment for Vignette C is a Set design task, not a scouting filter.
Zarathale finds the space with the right theatrical feel; Michael C. designs whatever
containment is needed — fence line, low wall, terrain modification. The constraint of
mob movement is always a design question to be asked and answered, not a reason to
reject a candidate location.

**Casting (Primary — shapes home base and Vignettes C, D, F)**
The creature selection for the collection shapes multiple departments' decisions.
Casting and Wardrobe must collaborate before any other department makes decisions about
home base. The collection's species list is Casting's first deliverable. Note: Wardrobe
answers the collection visual language question (see below) first, so Casting knows
what constraint to cast within.

**Wardrobe (Unblocks Casting)**
Wardrobe answers one question before Casting finalizes companion selections: does the
collection have a visual language? The options range from no imposed logic (the Allay
just likes what it likes) to a named thematic principle (all creatures with luminance,
all creatures from a particular biome category). This decision is the show's aesthetic
anchor for Home Base and should be made early.

**Sound (Lead on Vignette F — the comedy beat)**
Sound calls the timing of the punchline. Sound owns this beat's coordination. Lighting
and Casting respond to Sound's clock, not the other way around. Sound should name the
exact tick of the punchline in the intake conversation.

**Fireworks / Mira (Primary on Vignette E — the suspension)**
Vignette E belongs to Mira. She has the whole exhibit. Effects provides the altitude
context; Mira authors the experience. Mira and Effects must agree on player elevation
before either department authors a single line. Camera's orientation philosophy for
this moment must be confirmed in the same conversation.

## Vignette-Level Direction

### A — Home Base
*"The collection begins here. So does everything."*

The Allay is at center. It is not performing. Two or more collected creatures are present —
chosen and visually coherent per Wardrobe's collection language decision. The player's
first impression of the collection happens here and is set for the whole show.

Sprite speaks once at opening. The register: a brief annotation in a naturalist's journal.
Not welcome speech. Not explanation. Just the first entry. Something was noticed. It's
being written down.

Camera orients the player toward the Allay on arrival. Then releases.

The Allay may have an item in hand on arrival — a small visual detail implying it has been
busy. This is Casting's optional call; if used, Sprite may or may not reference it. The
detail is available; it doesn't need to be narrated to work.

Each A-section return is a brief beat — not a re-introduction. The ambient bed may already
be running. Sprite may or may not speak on return. When Sprite does speak at a return, it
notices something small about the Allay, not something about the vignette just seen.

### B — "The Still Water"
*"We found this place at dusk. We have not been able to explain the feeling."*

The player has agency here. No events take the player's attention — the exhibit IS the
place. Lighting establishes the time-of-day. Sound establishes the ambient bed: the water,
present and specific.

Sprite: one line, or none. Sound and Camera: establish and step back.

This vignette succeeds if the player notices something on their own. Every department is
present in B only long enough to hand the space to the player. Then they exit. If any
event fires after the first 60–90 ticks of B, it needs a very clear reason to be there.

Duration: shorter than it feels. Set the TP-home trigger on a generous timer so the player
has time to turn around once.

### C — "The Theater"
*"We observed this behavior on three separate occasions."*

A creature pair is already present when the player arrives. Camera orients the player
toward the stage. What happens next belongs to the AI.

Choreography stages the pair in positions that create the best opening image — near each
other, loosely facing the player's arrival direction — and then releases them. If entities
wander out of frame, that is a containment design question: Set answers it by building
what's needed. The constraint of mob movement is defined and designed for, not scouted for.

Sound scores what actually happens, not what was anticipated. Leave room for the AI's
behavior to be the show's content.

Sprite says nothing. The Watch question: *what happened?* Document the answer in the
revision log.

### D — "The Nether Valley"
*"This one lives here. It does not thrive anywhere else."*

The Strider is the exhibit. It should be visible immediately on player arrival. Camera
orients toward the Strider. Lighting does not adjust — the nether has its own ambient
register, and that register is the point. Whatever nether biome Set finds (basalt delta,
soul sand valley, nether wastes) has its own atmosphere; Lighting documents what it is,
not how to change it.

Effects uses near-ground levitation (2–4 blocks above the safe floor — exact after Set
confirms ceiling clearance) so the player feels slightly above the terrain. Visitor, not
resident.

One Sprite line if warranted. The Strider's AI-generated ambient sound is part of the
score; Sound does not try to score over it.

### E — "The Suspension"
*"Step off. You won't fall."*

This vignette is the show's most technically coordinated moment and its most ambitious
sensation. The player is elevated 15–25 blocks above arrival. Mira fires below and around
them. The intent is not to watch fireworks — it is to be inside them.

**The altitude contract:** Effects proposes the target altitude (18–22 blocks above arrival
is the target range). Mira confirms and calibrates all y_offsets from that number. Camera
answers its orientation philosophy — does the player face into the sky, face downward into
the burst, or hold arrival facing? The facing decision and the altitude number are coupled;
confirm together. Lock both at Gate 2 intake before anyone authors YAML.

Camera's FACE event, if used, fires during the levitation climb — not after reaching altitude.
The reorientation and the lift work together.

Mira's fireworks arc has shape: sparse entrance, building density, warm peak, falling away,
silence. The silence after the last burst is part of the vignette. Hold it.

Sprite says one line, maximum — the line that earns the view. Or nothing.

### F — "The Contraption"
*"We found this already built. We have not found anyone who built it."*

A redstone contraption exists in this space. The show activates it mid-vignette.

The design principle: small, specific, slightly absurd. A door that opens to nothing. A
piston that places one flower. A chicken that was waiting there. The comedy is in the gap
between the setup and the scale of the payoff. The contraption must not try to be impressive.
It must try to be exactly one thing.

The sequence: Sprite builds mild expectation (one line, pre-reveal). Sound and Lighting
hold the space. Then: the REDSTONE trigger fires and the comedy hit lands on the same tick.
The two events are one event to the player. Stage Management owns this alignment.

After the reveal: a beat of silence. The punchline creature, if present, does whatever it
does. Then, if warranted, Sprite speaks — one word, or one observation, spoken with the
gravity of a discovery. Or nothing. The after-line is the hardest line in the show; it
should be the last one written, after Set and Casting have confirmed what actually happened.

Casting's punchline creature is the joke — it does not explain the joke, it IS the joke,
or it's not in the scene. No wardrobe on the punchline creature. No fireworks. No camera
adjustment unless the creature appears out of the arrival frame — in which case a single
FACE redirect is allowed.
