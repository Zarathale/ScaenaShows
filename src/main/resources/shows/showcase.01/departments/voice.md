# Voice — showcase.01 "Preparing for Battle"

## Status

**Script v1 locked — 2026-03-28.** See `direction/script-v1.md`.

Full line set drafted and confirmed: 39 lines across 13 scenes (home base open +
6 expeditions + 5 intermediate returns + finale). The register, the ambiguous-address
structure, and the finale beat are all confirmed. Lines are ready for in-game test.

TBA flags in script-v1 are minor — a handful of departure lines reference location
character (forge, road, threshold) that may update once Set delivers coordinates.
All other lines are location-agnostic and survive scouting without revision.

**Dramatic irony — confirmed and locked:**
The Armorer is preparing the Vindicator to fight the player. The player does not know
this until the finale. Voice's "you" is structurally ambiguous throughout — addressed
to the Vindicator, received by the player. The finale resolves it warmly:
*"I'll leave you two to it."*

## The Companion's Voice

The Armorer Villager writes a procurement log. They are recording craft decisions,
not performing for an audience. Everything they say is specific to the armor, to this
Vindicator, to the work at hand — and none of it states the devotion directly. The
devotion is in the precision. The care is in the knowing.

### The register

**Craft knowledge as intimacy.** This mob has measured the Vindicator. They know the
shoulder width, the grip preference, where extra protection is needed. Every line
they write carries this knowledge in the details — not in statements about the
relationship, but in the specificity of what they choose and why.

The line that works: *"The chestplate runs wide at the shoulders — I measured twice."*
That's the whole relationship in one observation. No explanation needed.

The line that doesn't work: *"I'm doing this because they need it."* That's narration,
not procurement. The Armorer doesn't explain why they care. They demonstrate it.

### What the Armorer does not do

- Does not name the Vindicator or describe them directly before the reveal
- Does not say "for the battle" or any variation that explains the stakes
- Does not comment on being a small or unlikely procurement agent for this task
- Does not editorialize the devotion, the danger, or the outcome
- Does not recap expeditions on A-section returns

### Tonal texture per expedition

The Armorer's register shifts slightly across the show — not dramatically, but the
cumulative weight of six expeditions is felt. Early scenes are purposeful and forward.
Middle scenes are in-the-work. The weapon scene may be the Armorer's one moment of
stillness. The final expedition is the Armorer who knows they're almost done.

## Line Budget

One line per scene, sometimes none. The armor stand does the narrative accumulation.
Voice does not narrate that accumulation.

### Per-scene guidance

**A — Home Base (opening)**
The show's most important line. Establishes the Armorer's register for everything that
follows. Must imply the full scope of the mission without stating it.

The direction: something specific about the first expedition or the first piece — said
with the familiarity of someone who has done this before and knows exactly what they're
doing.

Draft direction (not final copy — to be written after Set confirms locations):
The line should land the player in the middle of a thought already in progress. The
Armorer is not introducing the show. They are already doing the show.

**B — Scene 1: The Helmet**
The Armorer is fresh. First expedition. One line, or none. Something about the helmet,
or about where they're going, that is specific and unadorned. The show is just beginning;
the voice should feel like it too. Forward motion, no ceremony.

**C — Scene 2: The Chestplate**
The Armorer is deeper in. The chestplate is the heaviest piece — Voice may acknowledge
weight literally (the object) or leave it between the lines. One line with slightly more
gravity than Scene B. The forge or nether setting can inform the line's texture —
something about heat, or about what the chestplate needs to do.

**D — Scene 3: The Leggings**
Middle of the show. The Armorer is in the rhythm of the work. This line has the option
of being the most routine — which is its own kind of statement. A functional observation
about the leggings, or the road, or the distance. Or silence. Mid-show silence says:
*the work continues, and it doesn't need annotation.*

**E — Scene 4: The Boots**
Something specific about the terrain or about what the boots protect against. Tactile.
The Armorer noticed something about the ground here that reminded them of the Vindicator's
needs. One brief observation.

**F — Scene 5: The Weapon**
The show's most weighted stop. Two options, both valid:

*Option 1 — silence.* The location does the work. The Armorer picks up the axe and
leaves without saying anything. This is the show's most powerful use of silence. It
implies the weight of the decision without making it explicit.

*Option 2 — one word or one line.* If a line, it is about the weapon specifically — not
about the Vindicator, not about battle, but about the axe: its weight, its balance, why
this one. Something a craftsperson would notice. The emotion is in the noticing, not
the statement.

Voice proposes both options at intake. Direction decides after Set confirms the location.

**G — Scene 6: The Shield**
The last expedition. The Armorer has everything. One line, or none. If a line: it faces
homeward. The Armorer turning back with the final piece. Something that implies
completion without stating it.

This line — if there is one — sets up the finale. Don't spend it. The reveal is next.

**A-Final — The Reveal**
The Vindicator arrives in full kit.

The Armorer has one word — or nothing. If a line: it is the only moment in the show
where the Armorer can acknowledge the Vindicator directly. It should be private. Specific.
Something the Armorer has been holding that they only say now.

This is the last line written in the show. It is written after Stage Management confirms
the reveal sequence and after Direction has seen the Vindicator in the kit in-game at
least once. It cannot be drafted blind.

## Writing Process

Line drafts are written against confirmed Set coordinates and staging. Voice cannot write
the opening A-section line without knowing the home base character. Voice cannot write
expedition lines without knowing what each location actually feels like.

The line-writing session opens at Gate 4 (after intake closes). All six expedition lines
and the finale line are drafted in one session, reviewed as a set, and revised together —
not piecemeal. The Armorer's voice must feel continuous across the show, and that
continuity requires all the lines to exist at once before any of them are final.
